What is a Library?

By Bryan Sukidi

January 26th, 2024

SOMERVILLE, Massachusetts — I remember getting my first library card when I was 8 years old. I was on a school field trip, and after patiently waiting in line behind my 2nd grade classmates, I took the pen from the librarian and proudly etched my initials onto the back of my new, blue, Minute-Man library card.

My world had changed. Soon, I was devouring every Magic Treehouse book I could lay my hands on, surfing through the vast sea of Wikipedia, and constantly losing myself in the nooks and crannies of the Somerville Public Library.

I'm sad that I no longer experience the same rush of joy and excitement whenver I pick up a new book, but it seems like I'm not alone. Over the past few years, Americans have slowly been falling out of touch with the public library system. From 2000 to 2018, libraries observed a 31 percent decline in overall use, despite the fact that government, state, and local funding for them has increased. Additionally, the COVID pandemic and the rise in technology has led many Americans to spend significantly less time reading and more time on their devices, a trend that has remained steady since 2010.

If libraries have been losing popularity for the past twenty years, why do we continue to spend $13.37 billion dollars each year to keep our public libraries up and running? Are there better places this money can go to? Is it finally time to move on from these outdated institutions?

Historically, public libraries have played a key role in combating poverty and equalizing access to information across all social, racial, and economic groups. Part of the problem is that when low-income families have to choose between buying food, paying the bills, or covering medical expenses, books are often the last item that comes to mind — a harsh reality reflected in the 60% of low-income families in America who are unable afford books. This early disadvantage often translates into long-term consequences for students' academic prospects: one longitudinal study of 4,000 kids found that "students who were not reading proficiently by third grade were almost four times more likely to leave school without earning their diploma."

Without having to worry about obtaining school supplies, stable internet, or working through other issues at home, public libraries have emerged as one of the few places where young children from poor backgrounds can learn freely, exposing themselves to abstract ideas they may have never come across before. For adults, the fact that public libraries have remained public has allowed countlesss immigrants, refugees, workers, and families to access the same social and digital capital as their well-off peers.

Computers are the perfect example of this type of social capital that public libraries have been offering for decades. In a study conducted by the Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation, they learned that “44 percent of people living below the federal poverty line [have] relied on computers and the Internet at their public libraries” - more internet usage than any other socioeconomic class. Just 30 minutes on the computer could help a person send a job application, seek training, or begin a resume. These statistics underscore the value of libraries as a mechanism for promoting upward mobility; even though overall readership is declining, libraries help provide individuals with the resources and technology needed to thrive.

In my research, what I wanted to uncover was where libraries were failing. Money, to my surprise, wasn't the issue; public libraries have seen extremely strong increases in funding especially after COVID, and the average library now receives around $836,810 in government assistance.

But when I came across Sei-Ching Joanna Sin's paper on disparities across public libraries, I was intrigued at the fact that lower-income, rural communities tended to have "shorter hours, less staff and programs, and [a] smaller collection size” than more urban neighborhoods. Her work was published in 2009. Would I find the same social inequities in the data from 2019 if I looked at poverty, race, and class?

Figure 1: Analysis of the programs hosted and attended by public libraries located in the 100 most White and 100 most Black neighborhoods.

After combining racial and socioeconomic data from U.S. Census with information from the 2019 Public Library Survey, I discovered that not only do public libraries in the most concentrated Black communities offer 5.2x more children and young adult programs than majority White neighborhoods, but they also receive about 6x as much attendance. In 2019 alone, an average of 30,000 people attended these programs in libraries located in the most Black communities.

When I looked at race, household income, and poverty rate, each identifier told the same story, but in its own distinct voice: low-income communities stand to benefit the most from community building programs hosted at public libraries, and America, unfortunately, is missing out on a lot of this potential. If you want to see it, just look at the map for yourself.

I fully recognize that there are likely disparities that I still missed, inequities that I didn't address, and nuances that I failed to capture, and yet still, the results speak for themselves. Seeing public libraries as simply places to store, lend, and borrow books is the wrong way to look at them. Buying more books will not solve the problem of educational and informational inequity. People don't just rely on libraries for the books. They seek the community and the resources they haven't been able to find, too caught up in the hustle and bustle of working multiple jobs, trying to raise families, and struggling all along the way.

To address the severe educational inequities across all social groups, we need to start by allocating more funding to K-8 schools specifically so that teachers can set up their own libraries inside their classrooms. The current problem is that many low-income parents aren't able to take days off from work, and so finding time to go to the library can pose as a serious challenge. If the goal is to put books in the hands of young kids - particularly low-income ones - the ideal solution would be to bring the books to them - not the other way around.

At the core of this, however, I want to reimagine what a "library" is. Almost every public library hosts these types of programs, but these programs feel only secondary to the library's "true purpose" of distributing books. There is an untapped potential waiting in these book-centered institutions.

Imagine if every public library was turned in a Community Center, where books were only one example of the many services a hub like this would offer: a community fridge to help support food-insecure families, translation services for immigrants and refugees, career-building workshops to help job seekers craft their own resumes.

If our goal is to help lift these underresourced communities out of poverty and into prosperity, eliminating the barriers to education and wellbeing is our first and foremost priority. For years, public libraries have contributed to the war against economic inequality by providing quality resources, support systems, and knowledge to the communites who have come to depend on them the most.
